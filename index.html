Parasaurolophus walkeri
200 University Avenue West
CREATURE BIO

Introduction: Parasaurolophus walkeri, meaning “near crested lizard” in Greek was discovered by a field party from the University of Toronto in 1920 (Parks, 1922). They found the fossil at the Belly River Formation in Red Deer River Valley of Alberta, Canada (Parks, 1922). William Parks, a professor of paleontology from the University of Toronto first described this new species in 1922 (Parks, 1922). Parks named the new species “walkeri” in honor of Sir Byron Edmund Walker, who was the Chairman of the Board of Trustees at the Royal Ontario Museum at the time (Parks, 1922).

"If our interpretations are right, then Parasaurolophus was just going through extra lengths to lower that frequency" (Bourke, 2018).

Quote by Dr. Jason Bourke who led a research team to examine the Parasaurolophus' unique tubular crest, which can create trumpet-like sounds. The team discovered that these dinosaurs had loops in the crests that can decrease sound pitch (Geggel, 2018).

When & Where: Parasaurolophus walkeri lived in the late Cretaceous period, 65 to 75 million years ago when it was a warm and humid world (Evans et al., 2009). They explored and foraged in the wet and dense rainforest of what is now North America, which was part of the northern supercontinent, Laurasia (Evans et al., 2009).

Morphology: Parasaurolophus walkeri was a member of the Hadrosauridae or "duck-billed dinosaur" family (Evans et al., 2009). They were around 9.5 metres in length and had a crested skull that was about 1.6 metres long (Glut, 1997). They weighed around 2.5 tonnes and had a pair of short forelimbs and robust thighbones to support them when they were shuttling across the rainforest (Lull & Wright, 1942). Parasaurolophus walkeri was an ornithischian or “bird-hipped” dinosaur and was able to walk on either two limbs or four (Strauss, 2017). When they were foraging for food, they walked on four limbs. However, they ran on two strong hind legs to escape predators such as the various species of Tyrannosaurids that shared the same habitat(Weishampel, 2004). There has been speculation that Parasaurolophus species had sexual dimorphism, in which the females had smaller crests than males, but it has not been proven (Strauss, 2017).

Diet: Parasaurolophus walkeri was a very large herbivorous dinosaur, so it had to spend much of its time eating. Since it was so tall, it could only eat plants that were four meters above the ground (Horner et al, 2004). These plants were likely gingko, pine needles, and leaves (Horner et, al). The plants were cut by their billed mouth and then sent to their back teeth for chewing (Horner et al, 2004). Parasaurolophus walkeri had hundreds of teeth to chew their food. If any teeth were lost or broken, new teeth would grow out shortly (Horner et al, 2004).

Unique Behaviour: Like all species in the Hadrosauridae family, Parasaurolophus walkeri were responsible parents. When the babies hatched, they were immobile so the parents would bring back food to the nest until the babies grew old enough to be independent. Parasaurolophus walkeri built nests to help their babies stay warm and to prevent rain from flooding them (Dodson et al., n.d.).

Evolution & Extinction: Parasaurolophus walkeri was a member of the Hadrosauridae family and its ancestry dated back to Iguanodontia, a common herbivorous dinosaur that lived during the middle Jurassic to late Cretaceous period (McDonald et al., 2010). 95 million years ago, Bactrosaurus and Protohadros appeared, with the morphology similar to Iguanodontia, but with teeth the same as Hadrosauridae. These dinosaurs were seen as a primitive species of Hadrosaur, the product of the transition from Iguanodon to Hadrosaur (Dodson et al, n.d.).

Finally, the first Parasaurolophus appeared 70 million years ago and they inherited powerful hind legs from their predecessors and developed crests (Dodson, et al, n.d.). Like most of the dinosaurs that lived in the late Cretaceous period, Parasaurolophus became extinct in the K-Pg mass extinction when meteorites and volcanic activities destroyed their homeland and food sources (Xing, 2014).

Fun Facts:

  • The teeth of Parasaurolophus never stopped growing! These dinosaurs used their hundreds of small teeth to chew tough vegetation. As the front teeth eroded, the back teeth will slowly move forwards and continue the teeth growing cycle (Strauss, 2017).

  • Parasaurolophus may have used their crests to help them regulate their temperature as fossil evidence hinted that these crests contained blood vessels (Sullivan & Williamson, 1996). The crest allowed these cold-blooded creatures to soak up heat from the sunlight during the day and dissipate it slowly during the night to maintain a homoeothermic body temperature (Strauss, 2017).

  • The crest of the Parasaurolophus was used for communication purposes! Palaeontologists have used computer simulations of their crest structure to determine that these dinosaurs were able to produce a deep resonating sound like a horn. Such sound was hypothesized to be used to communicate with other herd members in regards to danger and to signal mates for breeding (Strauss, 2017).
